Frequently Asked Questions

How is this different from the regular Anova Precision Cooker without WiFi?

The non-WiFi Nano costs $20-30 less but eliminates remote starting and app-based monitoring entirely. You'll need to manually set temperature on the device itself. If you meal-prep on weekends and can stand at your kitchen counter during heating, save the money. If you want to start cooking while running errands, the WiFi version justifies the upcharge. The heating speed and accuracy are identical between models.

What size water bath container do you actually need?

The Nano clamps onto any container between 5-12 quarts. Most experienced sous vide users settle on 8-10 quarts as the sweet spot—enough water mass to maintain temperature stability when you add cold protein, but compact enough to fit most kitchens. Avoid tiny 2-quart containers; they can't dissipate heat evenly and the circulator works harder. A basic stainless steel pot from any hardware store works perfectly; you don't need a specialized container.

Is the learning curve actually steep, or is sous vide simpler than people think?

Sous vide is easier than traditional cooking. You vacuum-seal protein, drop it in 167°F water for 90 minutes, pull it out, sear it for 90 seconds. That's the core technique. The learning curve exists only if you obsess over time-temperature combinations for different thicknesses and doneness levels—which is unnecessary when 167°F produces perfect medium rare almost universally. Start simple: 167°F for 90 minutes, sear hard in cast iron. Once that clicks, explore recipes. You won't break anything.
