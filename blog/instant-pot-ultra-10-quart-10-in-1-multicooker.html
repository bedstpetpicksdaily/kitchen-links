Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ The 10-quart capacity is a legitimate game-changer for larger households or serious batch cookers. You can pressure cook an entire chicken with vegetables, make 16 cups of bone broth, or prepare meal-prep portions for a week without running multiple cycles. That's efficiency that smaller 6-quart models simply can't match.
  • ✓ Ten cooking functions (pressure cook, slow cook, sauté, steam, rice cooker, sous vide, sterilize, cake, egg, and steamer) replace multiple single-use appliances. For someone with minimal counter space, consolidating from three devices to one is worth considering, even if each individual function isn't premium-tier.
  • ✓ The ultra model includes smart WiFi connectivity and a companion app, letting you start cooking remotely or monitor progress from another room. This is genuinely useful for planning dinner timing or adjusting settings without hovering over the machine—though it's not essential for basic cooking.
  • ✓ Stainless steel construction and a quality triple-layer bottom provide even heat distribution and durability that justifies multi-year ownership. The build quality feels noticeably solid compared to budget knockoffs.
Cons
  • ✗ The price point—typically $200–$300 depending on sales—puts this in premium territory. A standard Instant Pot Duo 6-quart runs $50–$80 less, and honestly, most home cooks never use more than 6 quarts at once. You're paying substantially for capacity you might not actually need.
  • ✗ WiFi features sound convenient until you realize they require consistent smartphone charging and app stability. The learning curve for the 10-in-1 functions means this isn't grab-and-go intuitive like a traditional slow cooker—there's real menu navigation involved, which creates friction for quick weeknight dinners.
  • ✗ Customer reviews reveal that the larger capacity actually slows cooking time for small batches compared to the 6-quart model, since the heating element covers a wider area. This contradicts the 'one machine for everything' promise when you're cooking for two people on a Tuesday.

Our Verdict

The Instant Pot Ultra 10-Quart is genuinely useful for large households, serious batch cookers, or anyone genuinely committed to pressure canning and meal prep at scale. At $200–$300, it's pricey, but the 4.3-star rating and 500+ reviews suggest buyers aren't regretful. However—and this is crucial—a standard 6-quart Instant Pot Duo costs half as much and handles 95% of home cooking situations perfectly fine. If you're shopping in July specifically for back-to-school meal prep or regular entertaining for 8+ people, the 10-quart makes financial sense. If you're a couple testing pressure cooking for the first time, buy the smaller model first. Don't let impressive capacity numbers bully you into overshooting your actual needs.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the 10-quart size really necessary, or can most people stick with 6-quart?

Most households don't need 10 quarts. A 6-quart handles everything from soups to roasts comfortably and heats faster for small batches. Buy 10-quart only if you're cooking for 8+ people regularly, meal-prepping 5+ days at once, or doing pressure canning. Otherwise, you're paying for cabinet space you won't use.

Does the WiFi feature actually work reliably, or is it gimmicky?

WiFi connectivity works as advertised—you can start/stop cooking and monitor time remaining from your phone. It's genuinely useful for timing dinner around arrival home. However, it's not essential for cooking quality, and some users report occasional app glitches. If WiFi features don't excite you, the non-WiFi 10-quart models are cheaper and equally functional.

Can I use this for pressure canning, or do I need a separate canner?

The Instant Pot Ultra includes a sterilize function and can technically process certain foods, but USDA guidelines recommend dedicated pressure canners for safety-critical canning. Don't buy this as a replacement for a proper canner if food preservation is your main goal. Use it for pressure cooking only.
