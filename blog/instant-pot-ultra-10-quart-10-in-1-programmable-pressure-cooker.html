Instant Pot Ultra 10-Quart Review: Worth the Counter Space?

By Best Kitchen Picks Daily • July 12, 2026 • Contains affiliate links

The Instant Pot Ultra 10-quart sits at a crossroads: it's massive enough to feed a crowd, but bulky enough to dominate your kitchen counter. With over 500 reviews averaging 4.3 stars, it has genuine users backing it up—but that middle-of-the-road rating should make you pause. This isn't a universal kitchen miracle. It's a specialized tool that solves a specific problem for a specific type of cook.

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ Genuine capacity advantage: A 10-quart unit cuts cooking time for batch cooking in half compared to running multiple 6-quart loads. For anyone meal-prepping or cooking for large families regularly, this is measurable time savings, not marketing hype.
  • ✓ 10-in-1 functionality delivers on basics: pressure cooking, slow cooking, sautéing, steaming, and yogurt-making all work reliably according to user feedback. The multi-function approach means you might actually consolidate some single-purpose appliances.
  • ✓ Durable stainless steel construction holds up: Users report consistent performance after 1-2 years of regular use. The lid seals properly, pressure releases smoothly, and the heating element doesn't degrade quickly like cheaper models.
  • ✓ Smart programming reduces guesswork: Pre-set buttons for common foods (rice, beans, poultry) actually work reasonably well out of the box, eliminating the manual time calculation many buyers dread with pressure cookers.
Cons
  • ✗ Takes up serious real estate: At 10 quarts, this demands counter space. If you're in an apartment, smaller kitchen, or already packed with appliances, this becomes a storage problem disguised as convenience. Small kitchens shouldn't buy this—it's not negotiable.
  • ✗ Heating time increases significantly with size: Reaching pressure takes noticeably longer than 6-quart models (sometimes 15-20 minutes), which partially offsets the cooking speed advantage. For quick weeknight meals, this defeats the purpose.

Our Verdict

The Instant Pot Ultra 10-quart is legitimate for two specific audiences: serious meal preppers and large households (6+ people regularly). For everyone else, it's oversized convenience that creates more problems than it solves. The 4.3-star rating reflects this honest middle ground—plenty of satisfied power users, but also buyers who bought too much cooker for their actual needs. The price point varies, so compare it directly against buying two 6-quart models; in many cases, you're paying a premium for convenience that only makes sense if you're genuinely cooking at volume. Skip this if you live alone, cook for two, or have kitchen space constraints. Buy it if you're hosting weekly family dinners or running a serious meal prep operation. There's no in-between.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much longer does the 10-quart take to pressurize compared to smaller models?

Realistically, 8-20 minutes longer depending on what you're cooking. Cold ingredients and larger volumes mean more time heating up. This cuts into your cooking speed advantage, especially for dishes that cook under 20 minutes. Fast weeknight recipes become less appealing on this model.

Can you use this for small portions, or does it waste energy?

Technically yes, but it's inefficient. Cooking two cups of rice in a 10-quart vessel means longer preheat time and more energy use than a 6-quart. If you're splitting cooking tasks between small and large batches, you'd be better served by owning both sizes.

Is the 10-in-1 functionality worth the extra cost, or should I buy a specialized slow cooker too?

The pressure cooking and slow cooking functions genuinely work. However, if slow cooking is important to you, expect lower performance than a dedicated slow cooker—10-quart models distribute heat less evenly at lower temperatures. The yogurt function is a legitimate bonus if you actually make yogurt; most buyers don't use it.

What's the actual difference between 4.3 stars and 5 stars on this product?

The lower ratings typically cite three issues: it doesn't fit in cabinets, the programming is less intuitive than newer models, and heating time disappointed buyers who didn't expect the preheat delay. These aren't product defects—they're size and physics realities.
