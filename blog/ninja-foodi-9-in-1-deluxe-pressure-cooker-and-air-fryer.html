Ninja Foodi 9-in-1 Pressure Cooker Air Fryer Review (2026)

⏱9 min read
By Best Kitchen Picks Daily • July 20, 2026 • Contains affiliate links

The Ninja Foodi 9-in-1 Deluxe sits at an interesting intersection in the multicooker market—it promises serious versatility without the premium price tag of luxury brands, yet costs considerably more than basic single-function air fryers. I spent the last three months cooking with this appliance daily, testing everything from meal-prepped proteins to desserts, and I've got specific thoughts on whether this 500+ reviewer favorite (4.3 stars average) actually justifies its investment.

Here's what matters right now: July is peak season for people renovating their kitchens and simplifying their cooking routines before fall entertaining. If counter space is limited and you genuinely use multiple cooking methods, this machine deserves your attention. But if you're stretching a tight budget, cheaper alternatives exist that might serve you better. Let's dig into the real performance data.

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ The pressure cooking and air frying actually work exceptionally well—not as gimmicks, but as genuinely useful functions. I made fall-apart pulled pork in 45 minutes using pressure mode, then crisped up leftover vegetables in the air fryer basket without transferring to another appliance. That workflow matters more than specs on paper.
  • ✓ Capacity is legitimately generous for a countertop model. The 6.5-quart pot holds enough for a family of five or serious meal prep, and the air fryer basket fits a full sheet pan's worth of food. I cooked 2 lbs of chicken thighs in a single batch without crowding, which most budget air fryers can't claim.
  • ✓ Build quality feels solid without being over-engineered. The stainless steel exterior resists fingerprints better than the plastic competitors I've used, and the control panel has actual physical buttons instead of touchy-feely capacitive surfaces that fail after two years of kitchen grease.
  • ✓ Temperature and pressure control is precise enough for actual cooking, not just 'set and forget' functions. I noticed consistent results across batches when making bone broth (15 PSI for 3 hours) and dehydrating beef jerky (lowest air fryer setting), which takes real engineering to nail.
Cons
  • ✗ The learning curve is real, and the manual reads like technical documentation instead of a recipe book. Most users will stick to 3-4 functions and ignore the remaining six, which means you're paying for features you won't master. My spouse still uses only pressure cooking and air frying after months.
  • ✗ Cleaning is more involved than a basic air fryer, especially the pressure valve assembly and the removable cooking pot. Those five extra minutes per cleanup add up to hours annually—not a dealbreaker, but a genuine inconvenience when you could own a simpler appliance that washes in under two minutes.

Our Verdict

The Ninja Foodi 9-in-1 Deluxe delivers real, practical value if you actually plan to pressure cook and air fry regularly. At its current price point, it costs roughly 2-3x what a dedicated air fryer runs, but you're consolidating two appliances into one. That math only works if you'll genuinely use both functions; if you're primarily after air frying, a $80-120 single-purpose model beats this financially. The 4.3-star rating across 500+ reviews reflects a solid, non-flashy machine that does its job well without innovation. Worth it for small kitchens or committed home cooks juggling multiple cooking styles. Overpriced for anyone just chasing air fryer trends.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does this compare to Instant Pot Duo Plus or other cheaper multicookers?

The Instant Pot Duo Plus costs significantly less but lacks air frying entirely—you'd need a separate air fryer anyway, negating the price difference. The Ninja includes both functions in one footprint, which actually saves money if you need both. However, Instant Pot's pressure cooking is slightly faster due to higher PSI (15 vs 12.5 on some Ninja models), though real-world cooking times differ by only 5-10 minutes. For pure pressure cooking alone, Instant Pot remains the cheaper option.

Can you actually use both the pressure pot and air fryer basket simultaneously?

No. This is the biggest limitation nobody mentions in casual reviews. You can pressure cook in the pot while the air fryer basket sits empty, or use the air fryer alone, but not both at once. This matters if you're cooking for a crowd and hoped to multitask. Plan accordingly if simultaneous cooking is your use case.

Is the 9-in-1 feature set worth the complexity, or should I buy simpler alternatives?

Honestly? Most households use two functions effectively—pressure cooking and air frying. The other seven modes (slow cooking, steaming, sautéing, sous vide, dehydrating, roasting, baking) are nice-to-haves that feel redundant if you own basic cookware. Buy the Ninja if you'll commit to mastering at least three functions. Otherwise, grab a basic pressure cooker ($40-70) plus an air fryer ($80-120) separately and save money while reducing cognitive load in your kitchen.

How long do the sealing gaskets and valves actually last before needing replacement?

Based on my testing and user reports, gaskets typically last 18-24 months with daily use, and replacement kits run $15-25. Valves rarely fail, but when they do, replacement parts cost $40-60. This isn't unique to Ninja—all pressure cookers have this annual maintenance cost. Factor this into your long-term budget if you're comparing total cost of ownership.

Does it really brown meat well in the sauté function, or do I need a separate Dutch oven?

The sauté function genuinely browns meat effectively, though the flat bottom pot doesn't concentrate heat quite like a proper skillet does. You won't achieve restaurant-quality crust, but it's more than adequate for developing flavor before pressure cooking. I've stopped using my Dutch oven for this purpose because the time savings (no transfer between pans) outweigh the minor texture difference.
