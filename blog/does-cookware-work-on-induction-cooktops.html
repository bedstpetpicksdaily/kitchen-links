The Short Answer

Induction cooktops require cookware with ferromagnetic properties, meaning the bottom must attract a magnet. Stainless steel cookware with a magnetic base, cast iron, and carbon steel all work excellently on induction. However, aluminum, copper, and glass cookware won't work unless they have a magnetic layer bonded to the bottom. Always test compatibility with a magnet before purchasing new cookware for your induction cooktop.

"For cookware to work on induction cooktops, it must contain ferromagnetic materials like iron or steel that respond to the magnetic field, which is why stainless steel with a magnetic base, cast iron, and carbon steel are ideal choices, while aluminum, copper, and glass cookware won't function unless they have a specially engineered magnetic bottom layer. This fundamental principle of electromagnetic induction means that consumers should always check the manufacturer's specifications or use the simple magnet test before purchasing cookware for induction cooking."

The Full Explanation

Induction cooktops operate using electromagnetic technology that directly heats compatible cookware rather than heating the cooktop surface itself. This technology creates an electromagnetic field that excites the iron molecules in ferromagnetic cookware, generating heat precisely where you need it.

Compatible Cookware Types:

  • Stainless Steel: Works if it contains magnetic stainless steel (typically 18/0 or 16/0 compositions). Higher-quality stainless steel sets are specifically designed for induction cooking.
  • Cast Iron: Excellent for induction cooking due to its naturally magnetic iron composition. Cast iron cookware heats evenly and retains heat exceptionally well.
  • Carbon Steel: Another ferromagnetic option that heats quickly and distributes heat uniformly across the cooking surface.
  • Clad Cookware: Stainless steel with an aluminum or copper core sandwiched between stainless steel layers. The outer stainless layer must be magnetic.

Incompatible Cookware Types:

  • Pure Aluminum: Non-magnetic and won't heat on induction cooktops unless it has a magnetic stainless steel base.
  • Pure Copper: Non-magnetic, though copper cookware often features stainless steel bottoms for induction compatibility.
  • Glass and Ceramic: Neither are magnetic, so they won't work on induction cooktops without a magnetic layer underneath.

The easiest way to determine if your existing cookware is induction-compatible is the magnet test—simply hold a magnet to the bottom of your pots and pans. If it sticks firmly, your cookware will work. If it falls off or barely holds, it won't function on an induction cooktop.

What the Experts Say

Industry experts consistently recommend investing in dedicated induction cookware when upgrading to an induction cooktop. Professional chefs and kitchen appliance specialists note that induction cooking offers superior efficiency compared to traditional electric or gas cooktops, with up to 90% of the energy transferring directly to the cookware versus only 65-75% for conventional methods. Experts also highlight that while induction-compatible cookware may cost slightly more upfront, the energy savings and precise temperature control make it a worthwhile investment for serious home cooks.
