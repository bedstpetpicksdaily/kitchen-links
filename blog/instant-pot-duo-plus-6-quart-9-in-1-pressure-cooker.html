Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Instant Pot Duo Plus compare to the standard Duo?

The main difference is the 6-quart size versus the standard Duo's 5.3 quarts, plus a few extra functions like sous vide (which works, but feels added as a checkbox). For $20–$30 more, you get that extra capacity and modest feature expansion. If you're cooking for four or more regularly, the larger pot prevents the annoying 'fill line' anxiety where recipes barely fit.

Is it worth upgrading from a basic stovetop pressure cooker?

Yes, if you forget things on the stove or hate constant monitoring. The Instant Pot sets it and walks away—your chicken won't overcook into rubber if you get distracted. The electric heating is more consistent than gas. Trade-off: cleanup is easier with stovetop models, and you save $80. Choose based on whether you value 'set it and forget it' enough.

Does the 6-quart actually cook faster than smaller models?

Heating time is slightly longer with more volume, so a 30-minute recipe might take 35 minutes in the Duo Plus. The time difference isn't dramatic enough to matter for most cooking—you're still saving 45 minutes versus traditional cooking. The real benefit is fitting more food per cycle, meaning fewer batches and less total kitchen time.

What's the realistic lifespan before it needs replacing?

Five to seven years of regular use before the heating element gets finicky or the sealing ring permanently warps. That's solidly better than budget models (two to three years) and roughly equivalent to premium models. At $150–$200, that's $25–$40 per year, which tracks reasonably against buying expensive takeout four times monthly.
