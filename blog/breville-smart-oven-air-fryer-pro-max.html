Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Breville compare to a regular convection oven and a standalone air fryer?

The Breville essentially combines both functions into one unit, which is its core advantage. Compared to a standard convection oven, the air fryer setting achieves crispier results using lower wattage and faster air circulation. Versus a dedicated air fryer, you get significantly more capacity and multi-zone cooking flexibility. The trade-off is size—it occupies meaningful counter real estate. For apartments or small kitchens, this might be a deal-breaker. I tested it alongside a $180 standalone air fryer and a conventional toaster oven. The Breville beat both in versatility, though the standalone air fryer marginally outcrispied certain items like frozen fries due to its concentrated heat chamber.

Is the smart app feature actually useful or just marketing?

Genuinely useful, though not essential to the core cooking performance. The main benefit I found was preheat notifications—no more guessing if the oven is ready. The live timer feature eliminates the need to peek or set a separate phone alarm. Where it adds real value: meal prep days when you're juggling multiple tasks. I'd say it elevates the experience from 'good' to 'frictionless,' but if you're budget-conscious, the same cooking quality exists in the non-WiFi Breville models for less money.

What's the learning curve for someone switching from a basic toaster oven?

Expect 3-5 uses before you stop second-guessing yourself on menu navigation. The display is intuitive once you understand the button layout, but it's not immediately obvious. I'd recommend watching YouTube walkthroughs before your first cooking session rather than learning by trial and error like I initially did. The manual is actually well-written and includes temperature conversion charts, which helps. After that adjustment period, operation becomes second nature.
