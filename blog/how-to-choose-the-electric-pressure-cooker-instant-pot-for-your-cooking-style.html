What to Look For

  • Size and Capacity Based on Your Household: If you're cooking for one or two people, a 3-quart model offers efficiency and faster cooking times, while families of four or more benefit from 6 or 8-quart versions. Consider whether you meal-prep in batches or cook fresh daily—batch cookers need larger capacities, while daily cooks may prefer the precision of smaller pots. Don't just buy the biggest model assuming it's better; oversized pots waste energy and take longer to build pressure for small portions.
  • Feature Set That Matches Your Cooking Interests: Casual rice-and-roast cooks only need basic pressure cooking, steaming, and sautéing functions, while adventurous home chefs benefit from sous vide, slow cook, and yogurt-making capabilities. Check if you need a built-in air fryer lid or dual cooking options—these are excellent if you're a gadget enthusiast but unnecessary if you prefer simplicity. Read through the preset programs and ask yourself honestly which ones you'll actually use versus which are just marketing fluff.
  • Control Interface and Learning Curve: Older models with physical buttons and simpler displays appeal to those who want straightforward operation without a manual nearby, while newer WiFi-enabled models with app control suit tech-savvy cooks who enjoy smart kitchen integration. If you have kitchen anxiety or tend to avoid complicated appliances, stick with proven, simpler models rather than the latest version packed with features. Conversely, if you love tinkering and want precision temperature control and detailed cooking data, the premium models with digital touchscreens deliver that experience.
