Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Breville's air frying actually compare to dedicated air fryers?

The performance is genuinely competitive. The key difference is capacity—the XL's full-sheet-pan interior means you can air fry larger batches without restacking or cooking in rounds. Dedicated single-basket air fryers heat faster in tight spaces, but for actual food results, the Breville matches them. The trade-off is speed for volume.

Is the smart/WiFi feature actually useful or just a selling point?

The remote monitoring is genuinely useful for meal prep scenarios where you're prepping other components while cooking, but it's not essential. You don't need WiFi connectivity to get excellent results—it's a convenience feature, not a core function. If WiFi capability adds $100 to the price, seriously consider the non-WiFi model.

Can this actually replace both a toaster oven and air fryer, or will I end up keeping both?

It can genuinely replace both, but only if your usage patterns align. If you toast bread every morning and air fry three times weekly, yes—consolidate. If you toast bread once weekly and never air fry, a $150 toaster oven is sufficient. The Breville wins when you actually use multiple functions regularly; it loses when it becomes a single-function appliance that cost too much.
