Instant Pot Duo Plus 8-Quart Review: Worth the Space?

⏱8 min read
By Best Kitchen Picks Daily • July 12, 2026 • Contains affiliate links

You've maxed out your 6-quart pressure cooker. Family dinners require batch cooking. Your meal prep ambitions have outgrown your current equipment. The Instant Pot Duo Plus 8-quart promises to solve this problem with sheer capacity—but bigger doesn't always mean better, and more features don't always mean smarter design. This review cuts through the marketing claims and examines whether this 9-in-1 behemoth actually delivers the convenience it promises, or if it's just more appliance than you need taking up valuable counter and cabinet space.

The Instant Pot Duo Plus 8-quart sits in an interesting market position. It's not the budget option, and it's not the luxury option either. With over 500 customer reviews averaging 4.3 stars, there's genuine data here—but that rating also reveals something important: this isn't a universally adored product. Some buyers rave. Others have legitimate complaints. Let's dig into what actually matters before you commit to this investment.

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ Capacity genuinely transforms meal prep workflows. An 8-quart cooker can handle a whole chicken plus vegetables, 12-15 servings of chili, or 6 pounds of pulled pork in a single cook. If you batch cook for a family of 5+, meal prep for the week, or preserve food seasonally (summer canning, anyone?), this capacity eliminates multiple cooking cycles. The inner pot holds serious volume without feeling cramped.
  • ✓ The 9-in-1 functionality actually works across pressure cooking, slow cooking, steaming, sautéing, sous vide, sterilizing, cake baking, egg cooking, and steaming rice. Unlike some multi-cookers that half-ass each function, the Duo Plus handles pressure cooking with legitimate PSI output and slow cooking that maintains consistent low-and-slow temperatures. The sauté function heats quickly enough for actual browning, not just warming.
  • ✓ Dual pressure settings (low 6.8 PSI, high 10.2 PSI) provide real cooking flexibility. High pressure gets tough cuts tender in 30 minutes. Low pressure prevents mushy vegetables in delicate dishes. Most competitors force you to choose one or the other—this gives you both without compromises.
  • ✓ Stainless steel inner pot resists staining and won't degrade from acidic foods like tomato sauce. It's also dishwasher-safe, which matters more than manufacturers admit when you're cooking multiple times weekly. The pot is genuinely durable and won't develop the weird discoloration issues some non-stick options develop after 200+ uses.
Cons
  • ✗ Physical footprint demands respect. At 8-quart capacity, this unit is genuinely large. Standard kitchen cabinets often won't accommodate it standing upright. If your counter space is limited (and whose isn't in July when you're preserving berries and pickling vegetables?), you'll need permanent placement or a dedicated storage solution. One reviewer specifically mentioned needing to clear shelf space—this isn't a grab-from-the-cabinet appliance.
  • ✗ The price-per-quart doesn't justify the jump over 6-quart models for most home cooks. An Instant Pot Duo 6-quart costs roughly 40% less while handling 90% of household cooking needs. The 8-quart premium targets specific use cases—large families, serious food preservation, commercial-adjacent home cooking—but regular home cooks will feel the cost without comparable benefit. Budget limitations are real, and this price tier excludes many people from consideration.

Our Verdict

The Instant Pot Duo Plus 8-quart is genuinely capable, and that 4.3-star rating reflects a product that works as advertised—but it's not a must-have upgrade for everyone. Buy this if you're cooking for 6+ people regularly, batch preparing weekly meals, or doing seasonal food preservation. The 9-in-1 functionality genuinely replaces multiple appliances, and the 8-quart capacity eliminates annoying cooking cycles. Don't buy this if kitchen space is precious, your family size is modest, or your current cooker handles your needs. The price premium versus 6-quart models is significant, and justifying that cost requires actual volume demands. The real question isn't whether this cooker works—it does—but whether your cooking reality requires this much capacity. Be honest about that before purchasing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much larger is the 8-quart versus the 6-quart Instant Pot?

The 8-quart holds exactly 2 additional quarts of capacity, which translates roughly to 33% more volume. For practical cooking, that means you can fit a 5-6 pound whole chicken where a 6-quart forces you to quarter it, or prepare 12 servings where a 6-quart makes 8. The physical footprint increase is also noticeable—expect to allocate permanent counter or cabinet space, not convenient grab-when-needed storage.

Is the 9-in-1 functionality actually useful, or is it marketing?

The pressure cooking, slow cooking, and sauté functions are genuinely reliable and frequently used. The sous vide, cake baking, and egg cooking modes work but feel like afterthoughts—most people won't use them regularly. The sterilizing function is useful if you have young children or immunity concerns. Honest assessment: you'll use 4-5 of those 9 functions regularly, and that's fine. The value is in the core cooking modes, not the novelty additions.

Does cooking time actually reduce with 8 quarts, or does it take proportionally longer?

Pressure cooking time depends on food density and heat penetration, not total quantity. A batch of beans takes roughly 22 minutes whether you're cooking 3 quarts or 7 quarts—the pressure and temperature remain constant. However, the cooker takes slightly longer to reach full pressure when full, adding maybe 3-5 minutes to total cook time. For batch cooking purposes, that's irrelevant. You're still cooking one large batch instead of two smaller batches, saving significant total time.
