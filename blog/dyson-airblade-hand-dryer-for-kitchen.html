The Dyson Airblade runs at about 75-80 decibels—roughly the sound of a vacuum cleaner or busy restaurant. During meal prep when other kitchen appliances are running, you won't notice it. But in a quiet kitchen at night, it's audible. This bothered me initially, but after a week you stop registering the sound. If your kitchen is open-concept and sound carries into your living room, test a unit at a showroom first.

Does it work well with soap residue or very wet hands?

Yes, efficiently. The high-velocity air blast (331 mph) cuts through soapy residue and handles dripping-wet hands without hesitation. You don't need to pre-shake your hands dramatically. Just position them under the sensor and wait 12-15 seconds. The only caveat: if hands are actively dripping water everywhere, expect minor spillage on the counter below—not a deal-breaker, but position it thoughtfully.

Is it worth it if you're the only one using your kitchen?

Probably not in terms of pure ROI. The paper towel savings shrink if you're drying hands 3-4 times daily instead of 8-10 times for a family. However, if you value touchless operation for hygiene reasons or hate the waste of paper towels on principle, it's still worthwhile. Single-person kitchens break even slower—maybe 3+ years instead of 18 months.
