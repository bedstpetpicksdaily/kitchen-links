Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between high-carbon stainless steel and regular stainless steel?

High-carbon stainless steel holds an edge longer (weeks vs. days) and sharpens more cleanly, but requires immediate drying to prevent discoloration. Regular stainless steel is more rust-resistant and forgiving but dulls faster. For beginners, high-carbon stainless is worth the minor maintenance trade-off if you cook regularly.

Why does blade hardness (Rockwell scale 54–56) matter?

This range indicates a blade that's hard enough to hold an edge through extended use, but not so brittle that it chips easily or becomes difficult to sharpen at home. Below 54, the blade dulls too quickly. Above 56, you risk needing professional sharpening equipment.

What's 'full tang' and why does it matter?

Full tang means the metal blade extends through the entire handle. This improves balance, prevents the handle from separating from the blade, and makes the knife feel more like a unified tool. Partial tang designs cost less but sacrifice durability and feel less stable during learning phases.

Is an 8-inch chef's knife really enough, or do I need a set?

An 8-inch chef's knife handles chopping, slicing, and mincing for 90% of everyday tasks. Beginners often overbuy knife sets; you'll reach for one workhorse blade daily. Start with one quality knife, then add specialty blades (paring, serrated) only if specific tasks frustrate you.

How often should I sharpen a beginner's knife?

High-carbon stainless steel blades like the Victorinox stay sharp for weeks of regular use. Pure stainless blades may need sharpening every 1–2 weeks. Budget for a basic sharpening steel ($15–30) rather than professional sharpening, which is unnecessary for beginner-level knives.
