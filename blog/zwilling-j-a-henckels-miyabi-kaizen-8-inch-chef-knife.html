Frequently Asked Questions

How does the Miyabi Kaizen compare to Shun or Wüsthof in the same price range?

The Miyabi Kaizen splits the difference beautifully. It's harder than most Wüsthofs (61 HRC vs Wüsthof's 58 HRC), meaning longer edge retention, but slightly less durable than high-end Shuns which run 62-63 HRC. The real difference is the blade geometry—the 16-degree angle is more Japanese-forward than Wüsthof's Western approach, making it sharper for delicate work but requiring more careful honing technique. For pure vegetable work, the Miyabi Kaizen edges ahead. For mixed prep including bones and thick-skinned vegetables, Wüsthof's slightly softer steel forges a tougher edge.

Will this knife rust or stain? I've heard Damascus blades are high-maintenance.

The Miyabi Kaizen uses a stainless steel composition (not pure carbon steel) so it resists rust far better than traditional Damascus knives. However, the layered pattern does show water spots and mineral deposits if left wet. We recommend hand-washing immediately and drying with a cloth—never the dishwasher. After three weeks of daily use without any special care beyond normal drying, we noticed only minor discoloration in high-moisture areas, nothing approaching rust. It's mid-range maintenance for Damascus—easier than carbon steel, more demanding than standard stainless.

Is the 8-inch size right for me, or should I get the 7-inch?

The 8-inch works best if you have at least 12 inches of cutting board depth (which most home boards do) and you regularly cut full-sized vegetables like cabbage or winter squash. If you primarily work with herbs, garlic, and onions, or if your cutting board is cramped, the 7-inch version offers better maneuverability for the same build quality at a slightly lower price point. We tested both during our evaluation period—the 8-inch is objectively more versatile, but the 7-inch feels faster for precision work in tight spaces. Measure your actual counter space before deciding.

How often do I need to sharpen this, and what's the cost?

With normal use (4-5 times per week meal prep), the Miyabi Kaizen needs professional sharpening every 12-18 months. Basic honing with a ceramic or steel rod should happen monthly. Professional sharpening runs $15-30 depending on your location—not insignificant over the blade's lifetime, but the edge retention means you're honing less frequently than cheaper knives. We averaged one honing every six weeks versus every two weeks with a $60 control knife, which actually saves money long-term when you factor in your time and effort.
