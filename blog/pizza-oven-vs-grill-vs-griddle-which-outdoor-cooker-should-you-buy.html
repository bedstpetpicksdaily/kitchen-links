Pizza Oven vs. Grill vs. Griddle: Which Outdoor Cooker Should You Buy?

Choosing outdoor cooking equipment is one of the most significant backyard investments you'll make, yet the decision is often clouded by conflicting advice and overlapping product categories. A pizza oven, gas grill, and griddle each excel at different cooking tasks, operate at different temperatures, and serve distinct entertaining needs.

This guide cuts through the noise by comparing these three distinct outdoor cookers head-to-head: what each actually does best, realistic maintenance requirements, space and installation demands, and which combinations make sense for serious home cooks. Whether you're a pizza enthusiast, a steak griller, or someone who wants to cook breakfast for a crowd, you'll find a clear path forward.

Pizza Ovens (Wood-Fired, 700–900°F)

Pizza ovens are specialized high-temperature cooking vessels designed to reach 700–900°F, allowing authentic Neapolitan-style pizzas to cook in 60–90 seconds. Modern units from manufacturers like Big Horn Outdoors feature advanced insulation and heat retention, meaning they stay hot longer and require less fuel management than older designs.

Beyond pizzas, these ovens can also roast meats, bake bread, and even cook desserts—but they're not designed for the grilling versatility of lower-temperature cooking (steaks, burgers, vegetables at medium heat). Setup typically requires a dedicated foundation and professional installation consideration.

Best for: Homeowners who prioritize authentic pizza nights, entertaining large groups, and want a statement piece that becomes the centerpiece of outdoor entertaining.

Gas Grills (300–600°F)

Gas grills are the workhorse of outdoor cooking, operating in the 300–600°F range and offering rapid temperature control via adjustable burners. They excel at grilling steaks, burgers, vegetables, and chicken with clear grill marks and smoky char, and they reach cooking temperature in minutes rather than the extended preheating time pizza ovens require.

Gas grills require propane or natural gas hookup and regular maintenance of grates and burners, but they demand significantly less physical space than pizza ovens and integrate easily into existing patios. They're also more forgiving for beginners since temperature adjustment is immediate and precise.

Best for: Families and casual entertainers who cook year-round, want fast weeknight grilling, and need reliable performance for a wide variety of proteins and vegetables.

Griddles (Flat, Even Heat Surface)

Griddles feature a flat, solid cooking surface that distributes heat evenly across the entire area, eliminating the gaps found on traditional grill grates. This design makes griddles ideal for foods that benefit from consistent contact with the surface: pancakes, eggs, burgers that don't need grill marks, delicate fish, stir-fried vegetables, and breakfast spreads for groups.

The flat surface also eliminates the dripping issue that comes with traditional grates—grease collects in a tray rather than falling through—and cleanup is significantly faster. However, griddles don't create the aesthetic grill marks or smoky char that a traditional grill produces, and they're less suited to foods that benefit from direct flame underneath.

Best for: Home cooks who frequently entertain groups, value ease of cleanup, cook breakfast or lunch more often than dinner, or want to prepare large quantities of the same item simultaneously.

Pros & Cons

Pros
  • ✓ Pizza ovens reach 700–900°F for authentic fast-cooking; grills operate reliably at 300–600°F for everyday use; griddles distribute heat evenly for large batch cooking.
  • ✓ Grills and griddles require minimal setup and integrate into existing patios; pizza ovens become focal points for entertaining but demand dedicated space and foundation.
  • ✓ Griddles are easiest to clean (flat surface, drip tray); grills offer versatility for proteins, vegetables, and sides; pizza ovens need less frequent deep cleaning thanks to self-cleaning high heat.
  • ✓ Combination units (grill + griddle) provide flexibility without tripling your equipment count or space footprint.
Cons
  • ✗ Pizza ovens require extended preheating (30–60 minutes), professional installation consideration, and dedicated foundation—not suitable for renters or small spaces.
  • ✗ Gas grills need propane/gas supply management, regular burner and grate maintenance, and can rust if not protected; griddles cannot produce authentic grill marks or smoky char.
  • ✗ All three require ongoing maintenance; pizza ovens and grills need seasonal weatherproofing; initial investment ($250+ average) makes the decision consequential.

Our Verdict

For pizza nights and entertaining: A pizza oven wins if you're committed to the investment and have the space. It becomes a destination and conversation piece. But recognize the 60–90 minute preheat and single-purpose focus—it's not grilling your Tuesday steak.

For everyday family cooking and flexibility: A gas grill remains unbeaten. It's faster, more versatile, easier to maintain, and works for everything from weeknight burgers to weekend entertaining. If you grill year-round or cook for varying group sizes, this is the practical choice.

For breakfast, batch cooking, or entertaining crowds: A griddle solves problems no grill can—imagine cooking 20 pancakes or 30 burger patties simultaneously on an even surface with zero sticking. Cleanup is also dramatically faster than a traditional grill.

The real move: Many serious outdoor cooks choose a combination grill + griddle (two surfaces on one unit) as a compromise that covers 90% of use cases without tripling space and cost. Add a pizza oven only if wood-fired pizza is genuinely part of your entertainment vision, not an afterthought.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you cook things other than pizza in a pizza oven?

Yes. Pizza ovens can roast meats, bake bread, cook vegetables, and even desserts. However, they excel at high-temperature cooking (700–900°F). Lower-temperature tasks like grilling a steak at medium heat are inefficient.

What's the temperature difference between pizza ovens and grills?

Pizza ovens reach 700–900°F (Neapolitan style), while gas grills operate at 300–600°F. This 300°F+ gap means pizza ovens cook much faster but are less suitable for temperature-sensitive tasks like slow-roasting or medium-rare grilling.

Can you use a griddle as a grill substitute?

Partially. A griddle cooks food evenly and won't lose small items through grates, but it won't produce grill marks or smoky char. If those visual and flavor elements matter to you, a griddle is a complement, not a replacement.

How often do I need to maintain a gas grill?

Gas grills require regular burner and grate inspection (monthly during season), grease trap emptying, and seasonal deep cleaning. Griddles are easier to maintain (wipe after each use); pizza ovens need less frequent maintenance but periodic chimney inspection.

Which option is easier to clean?

Griddles win. The flat surface and drip tray make cleanup simple—just wipe and empty. Gas grills require grate scrubbing and burner maintenance. Pizza ovens self-clean with high heat but need occasional ash removal.

What's the average cost and lifespan?

Average order value for quality outdoor cookers ranges $250+. Gas grills typically last 5–10 years with maintenance; well-insulated pizza ovens can last 20+ years; griddles are similarly durable. Pizza ovens often become permanent installations.
