Can You Use an Instant Pot as a Slow Cooker?

Yes, you can use an Instant Pot as a slow cooker—most Instant Pot models include a built-in slow cooker function that works just like a traditional slow cooker. However, there are some important differences in how they operate that you need to understand to get the best results.

The Short Answer

Most Instant Pot models feature a dedicated slow cooker setting that allows you to cook food at low or high temperatures for extended periods. This function works similarly to traditional slow cookers, though Instant Pots heat differently and may require some recipe adjustments. The slow cooker mode is particularly useful if you want a multi-functional appliance rather than owning separate devices. Keep in mind that not all Instant Pot models have this feature, so check your specific model's capabilities.

"While an Instant Pot can technically function as a slow cooker using its slow cook setting, it lacks the gentle, consistent low heat that traditional slow cookers provide, which can affect the texture and flavor development of dishes designed for eight-hour cooking times. For best results, I recommend using the Instant Pot's pressure cooking capabilities instead, which delivers comparable results in a fraction of the time while preserving the dish's integrity."

Sarah Blackwood — Professional Home Cook & Author

The Full Explanation

The Instant Pot's slow cooker function is accessed through the "Slow Cook" button on the control panel. Once activated, you can select either "Low" or "High" settings, and adjust the cooking time from 30 minutes up to 20 hours, depending on your model. This gives you significant flexibility for meal planning and different recipe requirements.

However, there are key differences between an Instant Pot operating in slow cooker mode and a traditional slow cooker. Traditional slow cookers use heating elements that surround the cooking vessel, creating gentle, even heat distribution. Instant Pots, by contrast, have a heating element primarily at the bottom, which means heat distribution may not be as uniform. This can result in slightly different cooking times and browning patterns.

Another important consideration is that Instant Pots reach operating temperature faster than traditional slow cookers. While a traditional slow cooker might take 30 minutes to reach full temperature, an Instant Pot in slow cooker mode reaches it much quicker. This means you may need to reduce cooking times slightly—typically by 15-30 minutes depending on the recipe and your Instant Pot model.

The capacity also differs between devices. Most Instant Pots have a 6-quart capacity, while traditional slow cookers often range from 4 to 8 quarts. This can affect batch cooking and family meal planning, so consider your household size and cooking needs.

Additionally, Instant Pots don't have the transparent glass lid that traditional slow cookers have, so you can't monitor your food's progress without opening the lid. This means you lose heat each time you check on your meal, potentially affecting cooking time.

What the Experts Say

Kitchen appliance experts generally agree that while an Instant Pot can function as a slow cooker, it works best as a hybrid solution rather than a complete replacement. Most recommend using the slow cooker function for convenience and space-saving purposes, but acknowledging that dedicated slow cookers still excel in certain applications—particularly for recipes requiring very gentle, consistent heat over many hours.

Cooking experts emphasize that recipes often need minor adjustments when transitioning from a traditional slow cooker to an Instant Pot. Reducing liquid by about 25% is commonly suggested because Instant Pots lose less moisture during slow cooking due to their sealed design. Testing recipes and keeping notes helps optimize results over time.
