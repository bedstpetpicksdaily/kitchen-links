What to Look For

  • Liquid Ratio Adjustments: Instant Pots use pressure to cook faster with less liquid evaporation, while slow cookers require more liquid since moisture stays trapped. When converting recipes, you'll typically need to reduce Instant Pot liquid by 25-50% when adapting to a slow cooker, and vice versa—add more liquid when going from slow cooker to Instant Pot pressure cooking.
  • Cooking Time Multipliers: A rule of thumb is that Instant Pot pressure cooking at high pressure takes roughly 1/3 the time of slow cooker cooking on low. Understanding this ratio helps you scale recipes accurately; a 30-minute Instant Pot recipe might translate to 6-8 hours on slow cooker low, or 3-4 hours on high.
  • Texture and Doneness Indicators: Instant Pots rely on steam pressure to break down tough cuts quickly and evenly, while slow cookers work through gentle, prolonged heat. Meat that becomes tender in 45 minutes under pressure might need a full day in a slow cooker, and the final texture can differ—Instant Pot results tend to be more uniform, while slow cookers can produce slightly softer edges.

What to Avoid

  • Don't Ignore the Browning Step: Instant Pot recipes often skip browning (since pressure cooking is fast), but slow cooker conversions typically benefit from searing meat first to build depth of flavor. Skipping this step with slow cooker conversions can leave dishes tasting flat, even if the texture is right.
  • Don't Use Instant Pot Liquid Amounts Directly in Slow Cookers: This is the most common beginner mistake and results in watery, bland dishes. Instant Pot recipes designed for minimal liquid evaporation will create soup instead of stew

    Frequently Asked Questions

    Can you convert any slow cooker recipe to an Instant Pot?

    Most slow cooker recipes can be converted to an Instant Pot, but not all. Recipes with delicate ingredients like seafood or fresh herbs may not work well, while soups, stews, and meat-based dishes convert easily. You'll need to adjust cooking times and liquid amounts, as the Instant Pot uses pressure and steam rather than slow, dry heat.

    How do I convert slow cooker cooking time to Instant Pot time?

    Generally, divide your slow cooker time by 4 for high pressure or by 3 for low pressure when using an Instant Pot. For example, a recipe that takes 8 hours on low in a slow cooker would take about 2.5-3 hours in an Instant Pot on high pressure, plus 10-15 minutes for the pot to come to pressure.

    Do I need to reduce liquid when converting to an Instant Pot?

    Yes, you should typically reduce liquid by 25-50% when converting to an Instant Pot because there's less evaporation in a sealed pressure cooker. Start by reducing liquid by one-quarter and adjust in future batches based on your results—you want enough liquid to create steam but not so much that your dish becomes watery.

    What's the difference between using high and low pressure for Instant Pot recipes?

    High pressure cooks food faster (roughly 3 times quicker than low pressure) and is ideal for most beginners and general cooking. Low pressure is gentler and better for delicate foods like eggs, fish, or recipes that might overcook easily, though it takes significantly longer and is less commonly used in basic recipes.
