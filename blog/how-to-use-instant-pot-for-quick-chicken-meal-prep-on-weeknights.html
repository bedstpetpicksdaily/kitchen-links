What to Look For

  • 7-in-1 or 9-in-1 functionality: You need a model that pressure cooks, sautés, and steams. The sauté function lets you brown chicken first for better flavor, while pressure cooking cuts traditional cooking time by 70%. Skip basic models that only pressure cook—you'll miss out on texture and taste.
  • 6-quart capacity minimum: A smaller 3-quart pot means cooking chicken in batches on busy nights. A 6-quart model fits a whole cut-up chicken or 3-4 breasts comfortably, letting you cook enough for dinner plus leftovers for next-day sandwiches or salads in one go.
  • Quick-release capability and reliable sealing: Weeknight cooking means you can't wait 15 minutes for natural pressure release. Look for models with intuitive quick-release valves and silicone sealing rings that stay sealed consistently—a faulty seal turns a 15-minute meal into a frustrating 45-minute ordeal.

What to Avoid

  • Don't skip the sauté step: Pressure cooking alone produces edible but bland chicken. Spending 3-4 minutes sautéing chicken and aromatics in butter or oil before pressurizing builds the caramelization and depth that makes weeknight cooking taste intentional, not convenient. The extra 5 minutes is worth it.
  • Don't cook frozen chicken with sauce already in the pot: This is a common mistake. Cook frozen chicken dry first, then add sauce during the last few minutes or after pressure cooking. Adding liquid before pressure cooking dilutes your flavor and can result in watery, underseasoned chicken. Plan for this in your recipe selection.
