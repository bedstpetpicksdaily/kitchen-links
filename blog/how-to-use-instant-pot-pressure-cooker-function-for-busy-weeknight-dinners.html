    Frequently Asked Questions

    How long does it actually take to cook food in an Instant Pot pressure cooker?

    Cooking times vary by food type, but most weeknight dinners cook in 5-15 minutes under pressure, plus 10-15 minutes for the pot to build and release pressure. For example, chicken breasts take 8 minutes, dried beans take 30 minutes, and tough cuts of beef take 35 minutes—all significantly faster than traditional cooking methods. The total time from start to finish is typically 25-40 minutes depending on your recipe and release method.

    Do I need to thaw frozen meat before cooking it in an Instant Pot?

    No, you can cook frozen meat directly in the Instant Pot without thawing, though you'll need to add 5-10 extra minutes to the cooking time. This is one of the biggest time-savers for busy weeknights since you can literally go from freezer to dinner table. Just make sure the meat isn't in a large clump and arrange it so heat can reach all sides.

    What's the difference between quick release and natural release, and which should I use for dinner?

    Quick release (manually venting steam) is faster and works well for vegetables and grains, while natural release (letting pressure drop on its own) is better for meat and keeps food more tender. For busy weeknights, quick release saves time but natural release for 10 minutes followed by quick release is often the best compromise for quality results. Most recipes will specify which method to use.

    Can I actually make a complete meal in one pot, or just the protein?

    You can make complete one-pot meals with protein, vegetables, and grains/pasta all cooking together, though timing requires planning since different foods cook at different rates. A common strategy is to add quick-cooking vegetables in the last few minutes or layer them on top so they steam above the liquid. Many Instant Pot cookbooks feature dedicated 'one-pot meals' that are specifically designed for this approach.
