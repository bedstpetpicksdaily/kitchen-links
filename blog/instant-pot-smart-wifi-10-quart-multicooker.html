Pros
  • ✓ The 10-quart capacity genuinely eliminates the 'batch cooking' problem for families or meal-preppers. One pot of chili, soup, or pulled pork feeds 8–10 people or creates four freezer containers at once—actual time savings, not theoretical ones.
  • ✓ WiFi remote monitoring actually works for busy schedules. Start a braise before work, check it's done while handling meetings, and dinner hits the table on time. The app is responsive, not laggy, and notifications alert you when cooking finishes.

Cons
  • ✗ The price tag is genuinely steep, and the WiFi feature adds $200–300 over the non-connected 10-quart model. If you're not actually checking your cooker remotely at least twice weekly, you're paying for convenience you won't use.

Our Verdict

The Instant Pot Smart WiFi 10-Quart justifies its cost if you're cooking for 6+ people regularly or meal-prepping in bulk, and if you actually have a workflow where remote monitoring saves you time. The 4.3-star rating reflects a durable, reliable appliance—not hype. But if you're cooking for four, already own a pressure cooker, or never check your phone mid-dinner, stepping down to the standard 10-quart (or even an 8-quart model) saves $200+ without sacrificing performance. This is the right choice for serious home cooks with the counter space and cooking volume to justify it. It's not the right choice for casual users or tiny kitchens.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the WiFi connectivity actually perform in real use?

The WiFi works reliably as long as you're on the same home network (or the Instant Pot servers stay up). Remote start isn't a feature—you can only monitor and receive notifications when cooking finishes. That's actually safer and more realistic than remote start. Response time on the app is typically under 3 seconds. Real users report it works as advertised, but it's a 'nice to have' convenience, not a must-have feature.

Is the 10-quart size really necessary, or would an 8-quart save money and space?

The 8-quart saves roughly $100–150 and takes up noticeably less counter real estate. For a family of 4–5, the 8-quart handles most meal-prep tasks fine. Jump to 10-quart only if you're regularly feeding 8+ people at once or doing full-week batch cooking. Otherwise, the smaller model delivers 95% of the value at a lower price.

How does this compare to buying two separate 6-quart Instant Pots?

Two 6-quart models cost about the same but take twice the counter and cabinet space, require twice the cleanup, and honestly feel more chaotic in a real kitchen. One 10-quart pot is cleaner to use and easier to manage. If counter space is already tight, though, two smaller pots might actually work better for your layout.

Do the preset cooking programs actually work, or are they just marketing?

The presets work consistently—rice comes out fluffy, beans are tender without being mushy, poultry reaches safe temperature without drying out. You don't need to fiddle with pressure levels. Most owners report using the preset programs for 80% of their cooking rather than custom settings. They're genuinely useful, not filler features.

What's the real ownership cost beyond the purchase price?

Electricity is minimal—a typical use costs about $0.15 per cooking cycle. The sealing ring wears out every 18–24 months with heavy use and costs $15–25 to replace. The stainless steel interior doesn't need special care beyond regular washing. Over five years of typical family use, you're looking at maybe $100 in ring replacements plus electricity. This isn't a high-maintenance purchase.
