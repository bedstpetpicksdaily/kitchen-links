Frequently Asked Questions

How does the 10-quart compare to the standard 6-quart Duo Plus?

The 10-quart gives you 67% more capacity and identical nine functions, but it's heavier (about 20 lbs vs. 15 lbs), slower to preheat, and requires more counter space. Real-world: a single batch of chili for eight people fits the 10-quart; the 6-quart forces a second cycle. The price difference usually sits between $50-$100, making it a worthwhile jump only if you genuinely need that volume regularly.

Is it actually faster than slow-cooking or batch cooking on stovetop?

Faster than slow-cooking? Absolutely—a pot roast hits tender in 90 minutes versus 8 hours. Faster than stovetop? Only for pressure cooking tasks like beans and tougher cuts. For sautéing vegetables or simmering sauces, stovetop still wins on speed. The real win is consolidation: one pot handles pressure cook, slow cook, and sauté without equipment switching.

Will this fit standard pots and cabinet storage?

No. The 10-quart measures roughly 15 inches in diameter and 10 inches tall with the lid. Most cabinets max out at 12-inch shelves. Plan for counter storage, a deep pantry shelf, or under-sink basement storage if space is tight. Measure your intended spot before ordering—return hassles aren't worth discovering it won't fit.

How many people should you cook for to justify this size?

Realistically, five or more regular eaters, or households that meal-prep weekly. If you feed two to four people most nights, a 6-quart handles 95% of recipes without the spatial footprint penalty. The 10-quart shines for large families, batch-cooking freezer stashes, and entertaining groups of eight-plus.
