The sous vide market has matured significantly, and the real question isn't whether immersion circulators work—they do. The question is which model actually fits your kitchen, budget, and cooking style. Anova offers two distinct WiFi-enabled options that occupy different market positions: the compact Nano and the more powerful Pro. Both boast 4.3-star ratings across 500+ reviews, but those stars mask real differences in heating power, footprint, and batch-cooking capacity.

This comparison cuts through the marketing to show you exactly what each model delivers in real-world testing, where they excel, and where they compromise. If you're considering a $150–$250+ investment in precision cooking, you need specifics—not generic enthusiasm.

Anova Precision Cooker Nano WiFi

The Nano is the compact choice—roughly 8 inches tall with a design that fits standard 12-quart containers without dominating your countertop. It includes WiFi connectivity with a responsive mobile app that lets you control temperature and timing from your phone. Temperature precision reaches ±0.1°F, making it accurate enough for proteins that require exact doneness thresholds (rare steak at 129°F, chicken at 165°F). The clamp mechanism grips securely without wobbling, and the device works in apartments and smaller kitchens where space is genuinely limited.

Best for: Small households (2–4 servings), apartment dwellers, and cooks who prioritize countertop real estate over batch-cooking capacity. Works well for weeknight sous vide without the industrial footprint.

Anova Precision Cooker Pro WiFi

The Pro represents the step-up option, designed for cooks who've moved past the novelty phase and want to integrate water bath cooking into regular meal prep routines. It delivers rapid preheating and consistent temperature hold within ±0.1°C (equivalent to ±0.18°F precision). The WiFi functionality and app control are genuinely responsive—not an afterthought—making it suitable for serious meal prep marathons. The heating power outpaces the Nano, supporting larger batch cooking and faster temperature recovery during consecutive cooks.

Best for: Home cooks committed to regular sous vide use, meal preppers who want to cook 4+ servings at once, and those willing to invest in proper vacuum-seal infrastructure and larger water bath containers.

Anova Precision Cooker (Non-WiFi Standard)

While the source material focuses on WiFi models, the comparison mentions a standard non-WiFi Precision Cooker as the baseline option. This entry-level model delivers core sous vide functionality without smartphone control or app integration. Temperature precision and cooking results are identical to WiFi versions, but you lose remote start capability and real-time monitoring from another room.

Best for: Budget-conscious cooks who don't need WiFi features, those with unreliable kitchen WiFi, and anyone who prefers simpler appliances without app dependencies.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the Anova Nano WiFi work without WiFi?

Yes, both the Nano and Pro include physical controls for temperature and timing without requiring WiFi. The app simply adds remote start and monitoring convenience—not a requirement for core cooking functionality. However, if you're paying premium pricing for a WiFi model, unreliable kitchen WiFi significantly undermines the value proposition.

What's the actual difference between Nano and Pro beyond size?

The Nano fits standard 12-quart containers and works best for 2–4 servings. The Pro handles larger batch cooking, has faster preheating, and superior temperature recovery during consecutive cooks. Temperature precision is equivalent (±0.1°F/±0.1°C). The Pro justifies premium pricing only if you're regularly cooking larger volumes or running meal prep marathons.

How long does it actually take to reach temperature?

Source testing doesn't specify exact preheat times, but the Pro delivers 'rapid preheating' with faster temperature recovery than the Nano. For practical purposes, expect 15–30 minutes depending on water volume and starting temperature. The Nano's compact design heats smaller containers faster than the Pro would, but once at temperature, both maintain ±0.1°F precision reliably.

Do I need WiFi on a sous vide cooker?

No—core cooking works fine without it. The WiFi value depends on your schedule: if you commute and want to start dinner before arriving home, it's genuinely useful. If you're cooking spur-of-the-moment weeknight dinners with full attention, physical controls are sufficient. Consider WiFi a convenience upgrade, not a requirement.

Is sous vide worth it for cooking just 1–2 people?

Yes, if you value consistency. The Nano specifically targets small households and delivers perfectly cooked proteins every time, eliminating the overcooked/undercooked risk of stovetop cooking. The real barrier isn't serving size—it's committing to vacuum-sealing and having proper containers. If those feel like friction, sous vide won't solve your kitchen stress.

What WiFi setup do I actually need?

A stable 2.4GHz WiFi network within reasonable range of your cooking area. 5GHz-only routers won't work. In large homes or kitchens far from the router, signal strength may struggle, limiting the convenience factor that justifies smart features. Test your WiFi coverage before purchasing if connectivity is unreliable near your intended cooking location.

Which model should I buy if WiFi isn't reliable in my kitchen?

The non-WiFi standard Precision Cooker. You'll get identical temperature precision (±0.1°F) without app-connectivity frustration. The premium pricing for Nano and Pro models only makes sense if you're actually using the WiFi features regularly. Don't pay for convenience you can't reliably access.
